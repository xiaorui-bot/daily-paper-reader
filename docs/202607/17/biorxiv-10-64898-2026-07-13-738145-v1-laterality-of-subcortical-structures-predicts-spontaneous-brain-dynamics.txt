# Abstract 

Subcortical structures play a key role in shaping cortical computation through 

distributed cortico -subcortical networks, yet it remains unclear whether individual 

diMerences in subcortical anatomy are reflected in resting -state cortical oscillations. 

We a nalysed resting -state magnetoencephalography (MEG) and structural MRI from 533 

healthy adults in the Cambridge Centre for Ageing and Neuroscience (CamCAN) cohort 

to test whether hemispheric asymmetries in subcortical volume predict asymmetries in 

cortical oscillatory power. Lateralisation indices were calculated for subcortical 

volumes and for oscillatory power across homologous MEG sensor pairs. Cluster -based 

permutation testing revealed frequency -specific associations between subcortical 

anatomy and corti cal activity. Globus pallidus asymmetry was positively associated 

with posterior alpha -band power lateralisation, putamen and caudate asymmetries 

were associated with beta -band lateralisation, and hippocampal asymmetry was 

negatively associated with delta -band lateralisation. These findings extend previous 

task -based observations linking pallidal anatomy with alpha oscillations to the resting 

state and demonstrate that distinct subcortical structures are associated with specific 

cortical frequency bands. Ou r results suggest that resting -state MEG captures 

functional signatures of cortico -subcortical organisation and provides a non -invasive 

framework for studying healthy ageing and disorders involving subcortical degeneration. 

# Introduction 

Subcortical structures are anatomically positioned to shape neocortical computation. 

Through direct and indirect cortico -subcortical loops, the basal ganglia, thalamus, and 

hippocampus contribute to the organisation of cortical activity supporting attentio n, 

action selection, memory, and cognitive control (Alexander et al., 1986; Buzs áki, 2006;

Haber, 2003) . Although these pathways are well described anatomically, it remains less 

clear how individual diMerences in subcortical structure are expressed functionally in 

neocortical activity, and whether such interactions can be detected non -invasively with 

magne toencephalography (MEG). 

A growing body of work suggests that hemispheric asymmetry provides a useful framework for probing these structure–function relationships. In a reward-related spatial attention task, Mazzetti et al. showed that volumetric asymmetry of the globus pallidus predicted hemispheric biases in posterior alpha modulation, suggesting that pallidal structure contributes to the control of neocortical alpha activity (Mazzetti et al., 2019). Extending this principle, Ghafari et al. demonstrated that asymmetries of the globus pallidus, caudate nucleus, and thalamus predicted attention-related alpha modulation under diMerent attentional demands (Ghafari et al., 2024). More recently, putamen asymmetry was shown to predict individual diMerences in pseudoneglect, linking striatal structure to spatial attentional bias in healthy adults (Ghafari et al., 2025). Together, these findings suggest that naturally occurring subcortical asymmetries are not anatomically incidental, but are related to lateralised neocortical computations. The present study asks whether this principle generalises beyond task-evoked attention to resting-state oscillatory activity. This is important because resting-state MEG oMers a clinically attractive route to probing cortico-subcortical systems without requiring task performance, which is particularly relevant for aging and neurodegenerative populations. Neurodegenerative disorders such as Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, and mild cognitive impairment involve progressive changes in subcortical structures, including the hippocampus and basal ganglia (Braak & Braak, 1991; Jack Jr et al., 1997; Li et al., 2022; Zeighami et al., 2015). However, structural MRI typically detects atrophy only after substantial tissue loss has occurred (Frisoni et al., 2010; Heim et al., 2017). Functional measures such as EEG and MEG may therefore provide earlier evidence of altered neural communication, although MEG is relatively insensitive to deep sources because subcortical signals are attenuated and distorted before reaching the sensors (Hämäläinen, 1993; Krishnaswamy et al., 2017).

Rather than attempting to measure subcortical activity directly, we tested whether 

subcortical structure is reflected in lateralised neocortical oscillatory power. We 

focused on hemispheric asymmetry for two reasons. First, asymmetry is biologically 

meanin gful: many subcortical systems show lateralised organisation, and 

neurodegenerative disorders often aMect subcortical regions asymmetrically (Kaasinen, 

2016; Minkova et al., 2017; Wachinger et al., 2016) . Second, asymmetry provides a 

within -participant measure that reduces confounds related to head size, global brain 

volume, and overall signal amplitude. If subcortical structures contribute to organising 

neocortical rhythms, then individual diMerences in lateralised subcortical volume 

should predict individual diMerences in lateralised MEG power. 

In this study, we analysed resting-state MEG and structural MRI from the CamCAN cohort to test this hypothesis in a large sample of healthy adults. Building on previous evidence linking the globus pallidus to alpha modulation, and the putamen to spatial attentional bias, we examined whether lateralised volumes of the globus pallidus, caudate, putamen, and hippocampus are associated with lateralised oscillatory power across canonical frequency bands. We expected the most consistent eMect to involve the globus pallidus and alpha-band activity, reflecting converging evidence from previous task-based MEG studies. We further tested whether striatal asymmetries relate to beta-band activity, and whether hippocampal asymmetry relates to slow-wave activity. Establishing these relationships in healthy participants provides a foundation for future work using MEG-derived asymmetry measures as functional markers of subcortical change in aging and neurodegenerative disease. 

# Method and Materials 

## Participants and Dataset 

Data were obtained from the Cambridge Centre for Ageing and Neuroscience 

(CamCAN) Stage -II release, an open -access neuroimaging dataset comprising healthy 

adults aged 18 –88 years. This release includes high -resolution T1 -weighted anatomical 

MRI and resting -state magnetoencephalography (MEG) recordings. A total of 590 

participants were initially included. After quality control steps for both MRI and MEG, 

532 participants were retained for the final analysis.

## MEG Preprocessing and Power Lateralisation Index 

Data were obtained from the open -access CamCAN dataset ( http://www.mrc -

cbu.cam.ac.uk/datasets/camcan/ ; Taylor et al., 2017; Cam -CAN et al., 2014). We 

analysed eyes -closed resting -state recordings acquired with a 306 -channel Elekta 

Neuromag VectorView system (102 magnetometers, 204 planar gradiometers). The 

dataset provided MaxFiltered and movement -compensated recordings, ensuring data 

quality prior to analysis. For each participant, a single fixed -length epoch of 7 minutes 

and 50 seconds was extracted.

MEG data. For each sensor pair and frequency band, Spearman’s rank correlation was 

computed between the power lateralisation index (PLI) and the lateralisation volume 

(LV) o f each of the seven subcortical structures across participants. The resulting 

correlation coeMicients ( ρ) were then normalised by converting them into t-statistics

These calculations yielded subcortical volume–sensor power correlation maps, producing a total of 7 (subcortical structures) × 2 (sensor types: gradiometers and magnetometers) × 4 (frequency bands) topographic representations. 

## Cluster Permutation Testing 

To correct for multiple comparisons, we applied a custom cluster-based permutation framework tailored for correlation analyses restricted to one hemisphere. For each correlation map, clusters were defined using sensor adjacency matrices specific to planar gradiometers and magnetometers. Cluster statistics were obtained by summing the t-transformed correlation values within each cluster. A null distribution was then generated by performing 1,000 permutations in which the mapping between PLI and LV was randomly shuMled across participants. For each permutation, the maximum cluster statistic was recorded to construct the null distribution. Observed clusters exceeding the 95th percentile of this distribution was considered significant ( p < 0.05). Figure 2

# Results 

We tested whether hemispheric asymmetries in subcortical volume were related to 

hemispheric asymmetries in resting -state MEG power. For each participant, lateralised 

volume (LV) was calculated for each subcortical structure as the diMerence between 

right and left volume. Similarly, power lateralisation index (PLI) was calculated for 

homologous left –right MEG sensor pairs across four frequency bands: delta, theta, 

alpha, and beta. In the main Results, we focus on combined planar gradiometers 

because they have a more focal sensitivity profile and are preferentially sensitive to 

sources close to the sensor array, whereas magnetometers are more sensitive to distant 

and spatially distri buted sources (Garc és et al., 2017) , we report the magnetometers in 

the supplementary materials (Supp.) . For each subcortical structure and frequency 

band, we correlated LV with PLI across participants and used cluster permutation 

testing to identify spatially contiguous significant sensor clusters.

The globus pallidus was significantly left -lateralised in volume across participants 

(Figure 1A; Wilcoxon rank sum p = 0.004). We next asked whether this pallidal structural 

asymmetry was associated with lateralised MEG power. A significant positive 

correl ation was observed between globus pallidus LV and alpha -band PLI over parietal 

gradiometer sensors (Figure 1B). This indicates that participants with stronger 

lateralisation of pallidal volume also showed stronger lateralisation of alpha -band 

power in the same direction. The frequency -resolved correlation analysis confirmed 

that the eMect was centred in the alpha range, with the significant frequencies 

highlighted in Figure 1C.

# Discussion 

The present study demonstrates that individual diMerences in hemispheric asymmetry 

of subcortical structures are reflected in frequency -specific asymmetries of resting -

state cortical oscillations. Across more than 500 healthy adults, globus pallidus 

asymm etry was associated with alpha -band lateralisation, putamen and caudate 

asymmetries were associated with beta -band lateralisation, and hippocampal 

asymmetry was associated with delta -band lateralisation. Rather than observing a 

single global relationship b etween subcortical anatomy and cortical activity, we found a 

structured pattern in which diMerent subcortical systems were linked to distinct 

oscillatory frequencies. 

The strongest finding was the association between globus pallidus asymmetry and posterior alpha-band power. This result extends previous task-based MEG studies showing that pallidal volume predicts hemispheric alpha modulation during visuospatial attention. Whereas previous work examined transient alpha modulation during cognitive tasks, our findings demonstrate that the same structural relationship is evident during unconstrained resting-state activity. Together, these studies suggest that the globus pallidus contributes to large-scale regulation of cortical alpha rhythms beyond task-specific attentional processes. We also observed robust associations between striatal asymmetry and beta-band lateralisation. Beta oscillations have long been linked to basal ganglia circuitry, particularly in relation to motor control and action selection, and abnormal beta synchronisation is a hallmark of Parkinson's disease. The present findings suggest that even in neurologically healthy adults, naturally occurring diMerences in putamen and caudate anatomy are reflected in the spatial organisation of cortical beta activity. The hippocampal findings revealed a selective relationship with delta-band asymmetry. Slow oscillatory activity has been implicated in long-range hippocampo-cortical communication during memory processing and large-scale network coordination. Although MEG has limited sensitivity to deep generators, our results indicate that anatomical asymmetry of the hippocampus is detectable indirectly through corresponding asymmetries in cortical slow-wave activity. Rather than reflecting direct measurement of hippocampal electrophysiological activity, these eMects likely arise through distributed hippocampo-neocortical interactions. A key aspect of this work is the use of hemispheric asymmetry as the principal analytical framework. Asymmetry provides a biologically meaningful measure while reducing inter-individual variability associated with head size, global brain volume, and overall MEG signal amplitude. This approach also aligns with evidence that many neurodegenerative disorders exhibit asymmetric subcortical pathology, suggesting that lateralised functional measures may provide sensitive markers of disease-related network alterations. Several limitations should be considered. First, the study is correlational and does not establish causal relationships between subcortical anatomy and cortical oscillations. Second, MEG primarily measures cortical activity, and the observed associations likely reflect network-level interactions rather than direct recordings from deep nuclei. Third, although the CamCAN cohort spans the adult lifespan, age-related eMects were not explicitly modelled in the present analyses and may influence both structural asymmetry and oscillatory organisation. Future studies combining source reconstruction, diMusion MRI, and longitudinal datasets could clarify the pathways linking subcortical anatomy to cortical oscillatory dynamics. In summary, the present study demonstrates that anatomical asymmetries of subcortical structures are systematically related to lateralised resting-state cortical oscillations.

Abstract 25 

The behavioural activity of freely moving animals is a confounding factor that affects the 26 

recording, analysis, and final results of animal EEG experiments. Along with the lack of 27 

standardisation in animal in vivo electrophysiology experiments, this could lead to huge 28 

inconsistencies , especially in the analysis of centrally acting drugs. Therefore, the main aim of 29 

this paper is to investigate the effects of behavioural activity versus inactivity on the 30 

multichannel EEG in freely moving rats. In a large samp le ( n = 116) of waking recordings from 31 

12 cortical electrodes (ECoG) in Wistar rats, we evaluated behavioural activity -related changes 32 

in the power spectrum, current source density, and power -based global functional 33 

connectivity (GFC) in a 3D rat brain mod el, according to the TOHOKU Rat Brain Atlas . The main 34 

findings were that behavioural activity induced 1) a robust power increase in 6 -8 Hz, peaking 35 

at 7 Hz with maximum changes over the parietal and temporal cortex, 2) an increase in gamma 36 

power (30 -80 Hz) across the whole brain, 3) a decrease in delta (1 -4 Hz) and beta (12 -30 Hz) 37 

power across the whole cortex. Changes were also localised in subcortical regions, particularly 38 

in the diencephalon/thalamus. The GFC analysis showed a similar pattern of power ch anges 39 

across the 6 -8 Hz, delta, and beta bands; however, GFC in the gamma band decreased. Again, 40 

the GFC analysis revealed changes in connectivity within subcortical structures, primarily in 41 

the thalamus . None of the measures was affected in the alpha band (8 -12 Hz). These findings 42 

emphasise behavioural state as a critical factor influencing EEG outcomes, with important 43 

implications for the standardisation and translational validity of preclinical neurophysiological 44 

studies. 45

Introduction 48 

Standards and guidelines for EEG recording and analysis in animal research are still lacking due 49 

to non -standardized electrode positions, recording parameters, electrode types, an d other 50 

methodological differences. As a result, the translational validity of animal EEG to human EEG 51 

remains limited. Compared with human studies, where data are typically collected from 52 

electrodes covering the entire brain, most animal studies focus on a specific region and use 53 

fewer electrodes. Consequently, they fail to reveal global effects, further limiting the use of 54 

modern computational methods for source localisation , as in human research (1). 55 

Other significant sources of variability are related t o behavioural activity (BA) versus inactivity 56 

and to open - versus closed -eye conditions. While in humans the resting -state (RS) EEG is 57 

usually recorded under clearly defined conditions of open/closed eyes , this is not possible in 58 

freely moving, awake animals. In such cases, differences between BA and RS during active, 59 

fully awake states, as well as the uncontrolled eye state, can lead to significant confounds. 60 

These effects are inevitably amplified when centrally acting drugs are used, as they t ypically 61 

alter the locomotor activity of animals (e.g., stimulants vs. sedatives, serotonergics, 62 

antipsychotics). 63 

Our group has previously introduced a 12 -electrode cortical electroencephalography (ECoG) 64 

system developed at the National Institute of Mental Health (Klecany, Czech Republic) and 65 

evaluated its performance for electrical source imaging (ESI) in rats. Recent advances in data 66 

collection, as well as in the development and adaptation of methods for 3D brain mapping (2), 67 

source localisation , and func tional connectivity in the rat model (1,3 –5), have enabled us to 68 

extend the current understanding of electrophysiological changes associated with BA and 69 

provide more translationally valid results. 70

In our studies, we have collected an extensive dataset usin g the 12 -electrode system, with 71 

electrodes placed over the frontal, parietal, and temporal cortices of both hemispheres, and 72 

a reference electrode over the olfactory bulbs. This placement, together with previous 73 

evaluations of source localisation and condu ctivity in rat head phantoms and forward models, 74 

enables the effective use of whole -brain inverse source localisation methods, including the 75 

localisation of thalamic components of event -related potentials (ERPs ) (1,6 –8). 76 

In addition to electrode -based func tional connectivity measures (4,5,9,10), we can now 77 

compute functional connectivity from source -localised data, such as power -based global 78 

functional connectivity (GFC). This metric, commonly used in fMRI research, represents the 79 

average connectivity of a brain area with the rest of the brain (11 –13). The EEG band -limited 80 

power (BLP) used for GFC computation is a strong correlate of the BOLD signal in fMRI (14 –16) 81 

and shows a similar connectivity structure to BOLD (17,18). Our recent EEG –fMRI integration 82 

re sults (19) underscore the importance of using both modalities, as connectivity structures, 83 

though sometimes spatially similar, can carry different functional meanings. 84 

In our previous pharmaco -EEG studies, we observed a substantial behavioural influence on 85 

ECoG changes induced by ketamine, amphetamine, and several psychedelics (3 –5,10,20). It 86 

has also been suggested that BA, particularly running, induces a pronounced peak in the theta 87 

band at 6 –8 Hz (theta peak) and a concurrent spectral power decrease in t he delta and beta 88 

bands (21) in rats . However, in that study, only four electrodes were used over the motor and 89 

parietal cortices , limiting the ability to localise the observed changes. 90 

Taken together, these findings motivated us to implement systematic co -registration of BA 91 

and RS conditions in all subsequent experiments. The present study aims to determine how 92 

BA affects the EEG in freely moving rats. Importantly, unlike many previous a nimal EEG 93

Animals 101

Spectral power analysis 167 

Preprocessed data were segmented int o 2 -second epochs with 75% overlap. Fast Fourier 168 

Transform (FFT) with the following parameters: 0.5 Hz resolution, voltage output, non -169 

complex, half -spectrum. A Hanning window (length = 10% of the segment, periodic, variance -170 

corrected) was applied, and seg ments were zero -padded prior to transformation. FFT was 171 

applied to each epoch, and mean spectral power was calculated for each subject , each 172 

electrode, and each condition (BA and resting state, RS). Differences in spectral power 173 

between BA and RS condition s were statistically evaluated using a cluster -based permutation 174 

test (24) implemented in the FieldTrip toolbox (25). To identify the neural generators 175 

underlying BA –RS power differences, we applied a source localisation approach developed by 176 

our group (1) . A homogeneous isotropic rat brain model (7) with realistic geometry and co -177 

registered 12 -electrode positions (Fig. 1C) was used to compute the forward model via the 178 

Finite Element Method (FEM) implemented in FieldTrip (26). The brain volume was sampled 179 

using a 1 mm isotropic grid. EEG time series were band -pass filtered to the frequency ranges 180 

defined above, segmented into 2 -second epochs, and projected onto the grid of equivalent 181 

dipoles (voxels) using the eLORETA algorithm (27). For each voxel, a single representative time 182 

series was derived as the first principal component of the three orthogonal dipole moment 183 

time series. The mean power across all segments was then calculated for each voxel in each 184

Global functional connectivity (GFC) analysis 190 

Global functional connectivity (GFC) was assessed for each predefined fr equency band .191 

Source -projected EEG data were first downsampled to 250 Hz. For each dipole, seed -based 192 

connectivity was computed using the orthogonalized amplitude envelope correlation (oAEC) 193 

method (18) . In this approach, the time series of each tested dip ole was orthogonalized with 194 

respect to the seed dipole time series to minimise signal leakage. The analytic signal envelopes 195 

of both the orthogonalized and seed time series were then obtained as the absolute values of 196 

their Hilbert transforms . Connectivity between the seed and each tested dipole was quantified 197 

as the absolute value of the Spearman correlation between their envelopes. To reduce 198 

computational load, the number of tested dipoles per seed was downsampled by a factor of 199 

eight . For e ach recording, a subset of tested dipoles was randomly selected prior to analysis. 200 

For each seed dipole, GFC was defined as the median connectivity across the selected tested 201 

dipoles, yielding a single GFC map for each recording and frequency band. At the group level, 202 

differences in GFC between BA and RS conditions were evaluated using a non -parametric, 203 

cluster -based permutation test (24,25) applied to the GFC maps for each frequency band. 204 

Significant t -values were interpolated onto the TOHOKU Rat Brain Atl as™ (28), enabling 205 

anatomical interpretation of functional connectivity changes associated with behavioural 206 

activity. 207

Seed -based functional connectivity (SFC) 208 

To further focus on the narrow -band theta peak (6 –8 Hz) frequency component, a seed -based 209 

functi onal connectivity (SFC) analysis was performed. Seeds were defined in regions showing 210 

the strongest source -level power (POW) increase at the theta peak. Based on the group -level 211 

t-maps, the right primary visual cortex (V1M) and the bilateral hippocampal fo rmation were 212 

identified as major hubs, since these regions exhibited the most pronounced BA –RS 213 

differences (V1M t ≈ 5.7; hippocampus t ≈ 5.7 bilaterally). Both seeds were therefore selected 214 

for SFC analysis to capture the complementary roles of sensory and mnemonic circuits. The 215 

connectivity of each seed with all other dipoles was calculated using the orthogonalized 216 

amplitude envelope correlation (oAEC) method, which minimises the effects of volume 217 

conduction and signal leakage by orthogonalisation of the t ested dipole time series relative to 218 

the seed. Connectivity values were estimated for each subject and condition, and group -level 219 

contrasts between BA and RS were tested using the same cluster -based permutation 220 

procedure as applied for global functional co nnectivity (GFC). This approach allowed the 221 

identification of large -scale networks specifically synchronised with theta -oscillatory hub 222 

activity in V1M and the hippocampus during behavioural activity. 223

Nf, where Nv is the number of voxels and Nf the number of frequency samples. Differences 229 

between BA and RS conditions were calculated, and the resulting thresholded maps were used 230

Spectral power analysis 243

activity (BA) conditions. A prominent increase in power was observed in a narrow spectral 245 

range from 6 to 8 Hz during BA, with a clearly identifiable peak around 7 ± 1 Hz. This theta 246 

peak was selected as a region of interest for further seed -based functio nal connectivity and 247 

cross -frequency coupling analyses. The grand -average power spectra across all electrodes and 248 

recordings (Fig. 1A) showed a global increase in both the theta peak and gamma -band power 249 

above 30 Hz. When spectra of individual electrodes w ere inspected (Fig. 1B), the theta peak 250 

was found to be most pronounced over parietal (P3/P4) and temporal (T3/T4) sites, which was 251 

consistent with the T -statistic map (Fig. 1D). In contrast, spectral power in lower frequencies 252 

spanning delta through beta bands was significantly reduced across most electrodes (Fig. 1D). 253

Discussion 396 

Behavioural activity in freely moving rats is associated with a characteristic, frequency -specific 397 

reorganisation of EEG dynamics that was consistent across power (POW), global functional 398 

connectivity (GFC), seed -based connectivity (SFC), and cross -frequency coupling (CFC). We 399 

first identified relative effect magnit udes across bands, then focused source -space analyses 400 

on those bands showing robust effects .401 

The most salient spectral feature of active behaviour was a narrowband theta peak at ~7 ± 402 

1 Hz. Source localisation placed this increase predominantly in posterior sensory cortices 403 

(visual, somatosensory, auditory) and hippocampal formation, with little or no engagement of 404 

the primary motor cortex. Such a topography argues against movement artefact and instead 405 

points to recruitment of sensory , associative, and mnemo nic circuitry during exploration. The 406 

association between voluntary movement and theta oscillations in the 6 –8 Hz range is well -407

established (32,34,35). Theta power and phase organise hippocampal and parahippocampal 408 

firing during navigation and decision -mak ing, scale with locomotor speed, and support 409 

sensorimotor integration and spatial memory (36 –40). Theta also accompanies intentional 410 

acts beyond locomotion, including frontal theta during consummatory behaviours such as 411 

licking (41), underscoring its broad role in coordinating action with sensory feedback. Our 412 

localisation to retrosplenial and posterior parietal fields dovetails with their established 413 

contributions to visuospatial orientation and memory -guided locomotion (35,38 ) and is 414 

consistent with our e arlier electrode -level indications of a parietal maximum for the ~7 Hz 415 

peak (4,20). 416 

Gamma power increased widely across the cortex and subcortex during behavioural activity, 417 

with prominent effects in sensory and motor neocortex, hippocampus –entorhinal area s, basal 418 

ganglia, and diencephalon. Gamma oscillations index local circuit engagement and are 419 

enhanced by arousal, sensorimotor processing, and active environmental sampling . In 420 

rodents, running amplifies gamma activity in the visual cortex, thereby improv ing visual 421 

processing during movement. Additionally, gamma activity in motor/somatosensory regions 422 

accompanies movement initiation and proprioceptive feedback (42 –44). Functionally, a global 423 

gamma upshift implies that the brain enters a computation -rich re gime in which fast local 424 

assemblies dominate information transmission and sensory –motor integration (43). Gamma 425 

often couples to theta such that theta phase modulates gamma amplitude, a mechanism 426 

proposed to organise fine -scale (gamma) content within theta cycles during active cognition 427 

(45 –47). The parallel upregulation of theta and gamma power in our data thus aligns with an 428 

“activated” cortical state , in which posterior sensory –hippocampal networks and subcortical 429 

integrators cooperate to support the exp loration and guidance of movement. 430

Countervailing decreases in lower frequencies were equally systematic. Delta power (1 –4 Hz) 431 

fell broadly, with maxima in medial prefrontal and anterior cingulate regions and extensions 432 

into insula, ventral striatum, and p osterior cortices. Delta is a hallmark of drowsiness, slow -433 

wave sleep, and pharmacological sedation; it diminishes with arousal and behavioural 434 

activation (48). Our delta suppression, therefore, may reflect a state transition from an idling, 435 

synchronous mo de to an alert, desynchronized mode characteristic of active wakefulness. 436 

Beta -band power (12 –25 Hz) and high -beta power (25 –30 Hz) also declined, with the strongest 437 

beta reductions observed in the diencephalon, hypothalamus, basal ganglia, hippocampus, 438 

an d sensorimotor/visual cortices, and a right -lateralized pattern in high -beta power .439 

Movement -related beta desynchronization is a robust phenomenon in both human and 440 

animal studies, indexing release from a status quo motor set and enabling movement initiation 441 

and updating (49 –53). The involvement of basal ganglia and diencephalic structures in our 442 

maps is consistent with beta’s generation within cortico -basal ganglia –thalamic loops and with 443 

dopaminergic modulation of these rhythms. Pathologically exaggerated beta in Parkinson’s 444 

disease reflects impaired dopamine transmission, whereas dopaminergic stimulation tends to 445 

reduce beta synchrony (54,55). Pharmacological evidence in rodents likewise shows that 446 

stimulants decre asing beta can accompany hyperlocomotion, while dopamine antagonism can 447 

elevate beta (56 –59). Mechanistically, the beta decrease during movement plausibly arises 448 

from increased inhibitory drive and desynchronization within GABAergic interneuron 449 

networks an d basal ganglia output nuclei, thereby facilitating flexible motor output (60 –62). 450 

The right -dominant high -beta suppression we observed resembles lateralized beta 451 

desynchronization reported with asymmetric motor preparation and may reflect hemispheric 452 

spec ialisation or subtle behavioural asymmetries (63). 453

high -beta, and gamma ranges. Suppression was strongest in thalamic/hypothalamic 457 

compartments, the BNST/ventral striatum, the amygdala, midbrain, and substantia nigra, and 458 

extended to the cingul ate, retrosplenial, visual, and motor cortices. This decoupling, despite 459 

concurrent power increases in higher bands, suggests a shift toward segregated, locally driven 460 

processing during active states: fast rhythms amplify within regions, while slow inter -areal 461 

coherence recedes. The pattern is expected for gamma, whose spatial coherence is typically 462 

local, and accords with the known breakdown of resting -state beta/theta coherence when 463 

animals initiate movement (64,65). It also helps reconcile discrepancies between natural 464 

behaviour and pharmacological hyperlocomotion: drug -induced states (e.g., NMDA 465 

antagonists, psychostimulants) can introduce additional, sometimes aberrant coherences, 466 

whereas voluntary movement alone primarily diminishes slow -band global co upling (4,33). 467 

These findings reinforce the importance of controlling for locomotor state in pharmaco -EEG, 468 

because active versus inactive periods differ markedly in baseline connectivity, potentially 469 

obscuring or mimicking drug effects (33). 470

ret rosplenial areas, primary somatosensory fields, and in hypothalamus/diencephalon. This 474 

focal enhancement supports the view that the locomotor theta rhythm coordinates a task -475 

specific communication backbone linking perception, spatial mapping, and state con trol. The 476

Conclusion 529 

In conclusion, behavioural activity reorganises rat brain dynamics along clear spectral and 530 

network axes. Theta (~7 Hz) and gamma power increases —localised to posterior sensory 531 

cortices, hippocampus, and subcortical integrators —coexist with widespread delta and beta 532 

power decreases, including a right -dominant high -beta desynchronization in sensorimotor 533 

and diencephalic territories. At the network level, GFC diminishes across broad theta, beta, 534 

high -beta, and gamma bands, indicating reduced long -range coherence during movement . In 535 

contrast, the narrow theta peak supports stronger, task -specific coupling among posterior 536 

sensory –parietal hubs and the hypothalamu s. Seed -based analyses from the visual cortex 537 

highlight theta synchronisation with hypothalamic/BNST and basal ganglia nodes, and CFC 538 

reveals a redistribution of theta –gamma coupling that emphasises medial prefrontal and 539 

accumbens circuits while attenuatin g coupling in insular and entorhinal/perirhinal regions. 540 

Together, these findings outline a coherent physiological picture: in active wakefulness, the 541 

rat brain shifts toward a locally amplified, selectively coupled, theta -anchored regime 542 

optimised for vis uospatial navigation, action selection, and state control. Beyond providing a 543 

behaviourally grounded baseline for pharmaco -EEG (4,33), this framework should help parse 544 

how neuromodulators and candidate therapeutics reshape the interplay of power, 545 

connectiv ity, and cross -frequency coupling across sensory, limbic, and motor circuits. 546
